The Attention Economy and Its Effects

#socialnetworks

#attentioneconomy

#bigtech

One of the most important characteristics that humans possess is their attention and ability to focus on things that matter to either the individual or the species as a whole. However, large social networking companies have spent hundreds of millions of dollars to capture and hold this attention because of an understanding of how valuable it is, in the most literal sense of the word. This is the basis of the attention economy, defined as the commodification of human attention, which has always been an important resource but has been exacerbated by the rise of the internet and social media.

In our everyday lives, the attention economy means that we are constantly bombarded with information so as to keep our attention. Whether it is intended to be malicious or not, companies understand how important it is to keep a user's attention. Take Instagram, for example. Meta, the parent company of Instagram, is a publicly traded company, and they have a duty to their shareholders to make more money. Instagram makes money through consumers interacting with the app, specifically, viewing ads. The more time someone spends on Instagram, the more ads they see, and the more money Instagram makes. Therefore, Instagram has a vested interest in keeping the user's attention for as long as possible. One of the ways that Instagram has done this is to implement more addictive content that keeps the user engaged with the platform. This was done with the introduction of the Stories feature. This feature allowed users to post pictures and videos that would disappear after 24 hours, meaning that if you wanted to keep up to date with someone, you would have to check Instagram every day.

This, paired with an "infinite-scroll" feed, has made it incredibly easy for users to open the app and get sucked into the platform for hours on end, viewing ads and making Meta's shareholders happy.

We absolutely need to protect our attention, and we cannot, under any circumstances, let tech. giants protect it for us. Currently, they are controlling our attention and there is no reason to assume that they can be trusted to pivot and protect it. We need to take back control of our attention, which I have actively tried to do by implementing a few strategies. I have turned off all notifications on my phone, and, on my laptop, I have paid for an app called Focus ↗ which is a website and application blocker that I have used to keep me on track. It is incredibly effective, and my daily screen time has been reduced to minutes instead of hours.

I think that media multitasking is probably the most draining and damaging action that we can do to our attention. This idea can be described as context switching ↗, and it is the act of switching between projects or tasks of varying depths to another project or task. Not only does it reduce productivity, but it also reduces the quality of work that is being done, and it makes you feel more tired and less focused. I used to feel incredibly drained at the end of the week, but since I have stopped context switching so frequently, I have felt more energized and focused on my work.

In conclusion, the attention economy is a real, tangible thing that we need to be aware of. We need to take back control of our attention and not trust tech. giants to protect our attention. This can be done by reducing context switching and using tools to stay on track, or at least not getting sucked into social media.
