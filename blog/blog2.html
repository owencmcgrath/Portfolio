My Experience with a Digital Detox

I rarely feel overwhelmed by the number of emails or messages that I get, but I think that is entirely intentional. I never sign up for email newsletters unless I am convinced that I am interested in the content being shown. I also never give out my phone number unless I am sure that I am OK with the person contacting me. I have a very strict policy on who I let into my life, and that contributes to why I am not overwhelmed by hyperactive communication channels. It's important to note that I tell everyone to call me, instead of texting me, if they have something urgent to say. I find that people are less likely to call me, which is good, because I don't want to be bothered by constant notifications. I take pride in being unreachable via text/email. I also only check my email once a day, over lunch, which means that I am rarely overwhelmed by the number of emails that I get.

Because of this, I don't want to turn it all off because it's as close to turned off as possible. I don't have that many social media connections, so I don't feel lost without them. I am entirely in love with my alone time, where I can sit and read, or code, or watch movies. I don't feel the need to be connected unless I am the one seeking the connection. I don't think that there's a middle ground; humans weren't made for constant connection, and I think everyone in the world would be a lot happier if they were to just ditch social media. The creator of Studio Ghibli ↗, Hayao Miyazaki, said it pretty well: “Modern life is so thin and shallow and fake. I look forward to when developers go bankrupt, Japan gets poorer, and wild grasses take over.”

I hate to sound pretentious, but this challenge was not difficult for me. I spend such little time on my phone, already, that I just ended up reading a book that I had been working through recently. I also spent some time parsing through IBM's Quantum Computing ↗ course, which is a subject that I am just infatuated with. I also cleaned out some junk drawers in my apartment.

During this time, I was mostly thinking about how much I love my alone time and how much I love silence. I have also been mulling over some project ideas, so I got to flesh those out. I felt normal and just relaxed, which is something that I don't feel when I am connected to technology. This was a great opportunity for reflection, and I got to spend some time meditating as I began the challenge. All of this culminated to reinforce my abandonment of social media and my commitment to myself and my own well-being. I am so much happier without it, and I think that everyone should try to take a break from technology every once in a while, even if it's just for a day.
