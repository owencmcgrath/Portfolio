Cancel Culture and the Coddling of the American Mind

#cancel-culture

#american-universities

#sociology

In my high school, a girl I had grown up with and attended elementary and middle school with was recorded saying a racial slur in a way that was derogatory. She was ridiculed and labeled as "cancelled," ruining her life at our school with repercussions in areas unrelated to our academics. She, quite literally, fled the state, as her family felt pressured to leave the school. She landed in Canada and, as far as I know, restarted her life, including attending university there.

The heartbreaking part of this was that the recording was from years prior, when she was likely thirteen; old enough to know what she was saying, but not old enough to extrapolate the consequences that she would face if the recording was published. In the years between her saying the slur and it being released, she took considerable steps to try and distance herself from this part of her past. She joined racial activism groups, spoke up for marginalized students, and organized rallies at the beginning of the modern BLM movement. This anecdote has had a deep personal effect on my approach to cancel culture, so I will focus solely on that topic in this blog.

Cancel culture is fast-moving and nebulous, making it hard to put any concrete characteristics to it. George Packard ↗ described it to AP News ↗ as "a mechanism where a chorus of voices, amplified on social media, tries to silence a point of view that they find offensive by trying to damage or destroy the reputation of the person who has given offense." While this definition captures part of the phenomenon, cancel culture exists on a spectrum that demands our critical attention as media consumers and citizens.

While cancel culture emerged as a vital tool for collective accountability, it now demands critical media literacy skills to distinguish between justified consequences for harmful actions and the silencing of intellectual discourse. In our digital landscape where social media amplifies and accelerates public judgment, the ability to evaluate context, verify information, and consider diverse perspectives represents essential media literacy competencies that can transform how we engage with cancel culture phenomena.

I am not against cancel culture in its classical form. The original instances of cancel culture brought people like Harvey Weinstein and Bill Cosby to justice for their crimes. Social media provided a community for these affected women to speak about their stories, and movements like #metoo were spawned from the ability to speak out as a collective, rather than as individuals. This worked well, and several other movements were spawned from the success of #metoo. People realized that they could come together with a common goal and remove the power of an organization or person.

However, this is where the idea of cancel culture pivots from its classical stance of ousting bad people from power into a contemporary position where groups use cancel culture to attack an idea that they dislike. The most famous example of this is the University of California Berkeley (UC-Berkeley) riots which happened in 2017 after conservative speaker Milo Yiannopoulos was slated to speak on campus. Prior to the event, according to CNN ↗ , "more than 1,500 protesters had gathered at Sproul Plaza, chanting and holding signs that read: 'No safe space for racists' and 'This is war.'" After that, protests quickly turned into riots that would eventually cause $100,000 in damage to the campus and surrounding downtown.

This incident is not isolated, with another conservative speaker's speech being cancelled at University of California Davis ↗ after students formed protests to have the speaker barred from campus. These groups used their collective power to prevent a speaker whose ideas they disagreed with from talking at the university.

From a media literacy perspective, these scenarios present complex challenges. When UC-Berkeley students prevented Yiannopoulos from speaking, they exercised a form of collective gatekeeping, deciding which ideas deserve public platforms. This approach bypasses the critical media literacy skill of evaluating content based on evidence, context, and reasoned analysis. The university, traditionally a space for intellectual exploration and ideological debate, instead became an environment where certain perspectives were deemed unworthy of engagement.

The core media literacy question becomes: how do we distinguish between necessary accountability and problematic censorship? Effective media literacy involves the ability to evaluate ideas based on their merits rather than silencing voices we disagree with. It requires analyzing context, critically examining information sources, and developing the capacity to engage with challenging perspectives without immediately rejecting them as dangerous or harmful.
